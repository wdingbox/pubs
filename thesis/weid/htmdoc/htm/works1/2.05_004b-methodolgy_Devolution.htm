1. Biology Devolution

The term Devolution appeared in biology as early as 19th century during Darwin's times. It was an opposite theory against Darwin's evolution. But it was neglected by mainstream. However, it  is still valuable for today. Its key points are listed below:

  1. The species can change into a more "primitive" simple form rather than advanced complexity. (Bénédict Morel / Lamarckism, 1857)
  2. Contrasting modern humanity to the past,  there had been "Morbid deviation from an original type" for mankind. (Bénédict Morel / Lamarckism, 1857)
  3. The species diversity is the result of a purposeful internal drive or tendency to form improved adaptations to the environment, rather than randomly natural selections.  (Lamarckism and orthogenesis)
  4. Darwin soothed readers that evolution was progressive, and directed towards human perfectibility. The next generation of biologists were less confident or consoling. Using Darwin's theory, and many rival biological accounts of development then in circulation, scientists suspected that it was just as possible to devolve, to slip back down the evolutionary scale to prior states of development.(Luckhurst, 2005)
  5. An evolutionary retreat back to a state resembling that from which it began." (Peter J. Bowler)
  6. The devolution was predetermined by internal factors that organisms can neither control or reverse. (Hyatt )
  7. Complex organs evolve in a lineage over many generations, and once lost they are unlikely to re-evolve. The evolution is not reversible (Dollo's law)
