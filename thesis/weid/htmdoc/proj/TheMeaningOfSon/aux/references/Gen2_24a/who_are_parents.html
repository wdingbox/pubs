4. 'Father' and 'Mother' 
 
In the OT, the word 'father' and 'mother' first appear in Genesis 2 right after Adam and Eve met each other after they were created respectively by God. "For this reason (עַל־כֵּן) a man (אִישׁ) will leave (עָזַב) his father (אָב) and mother (אֵם) and be united (דָּבַק) to his wife (אִשָּׁה), and they will become one flesh (בָּשָׂר)." (Gen2:24 NIV) 
Gen2:24 is very special in the Bible. It is directly quoted by Jesus and occurs in the NT at least four times (Mat19:4-5, Mak10:6-9, Eph5:31-32, 1Co6:16-17).  It is the earliest scripture about the most complicated relationship among father and mother, man and woman. However, most people focus on the relationship between man and woman, rare people quest after 'father' and 'mother' due to preoccupied mindsets, for example, what are 'father' and 'mother'? are they two or one? are they the parents of man or woman or both? who are they exactly? The dictionary and the following outline of the usage can help but a little. (from blueleterbible)
 
Outline of Biblical Usage of Father (אָב)

  1. father of an individual

  2. of God as father of his people

  3. head or founder of a household, group, family, or clan

  4. ancestor

    1. grandfather, forefathers — of person

    2. of people

  5. originator or patron of a class, profession, or art

  6. of producer, generator (fig.)

  7. of benevolence and protection (fig.)

  8. term of respect and honour

  9. ruler or chief (spec.)

Outline of Biblical Usage of Mother (אֵם)

  1. mother

    1. of humans

    2. of Deborah's relationship to the people (fig.)

    3. of animals

  2. point of departure or division

 

 

Quotation Marks Issue

Gen2:24 is located between the Adam's saying Gen2:23 and the narrative verse Gen2:25. In ancient Hebew scriptures, there was no punctuation or quotation marks. Whether Gen2:24 is part of Adam's saying is not a problem for biblical scribers. It was not until after the middle of the sixteenth century when quotation marks became popular in the printed Bible translations, whether Gen2:24 came from Adam or not became a prominent issue. Also, because Adam's speech is a monologue rather than a dialogue, it increased the complexity of the issue. 

Most scholars believe that Gen2:24 came from Moses (narrator). The reasons can be summerized as following:

(1) Adam had no parents. He knew nothing about parents at that time. He could not say anything about parents. It had to be Moses. (Ellicott's Commentary)

(2) Jesus quotes these as spoken by God (Mat19:5, Mak10:6-9) to prove that these are from narrator rather than Adam (Ellicott's Commentary, Benson Commentary). Pulpit Commentary sais that "the statement of Christ (Matthew 19:5) does not preclude the possibility of Moses being their author;"

(3) The word 'therefore' (עַל־כֵּן֙) infers that Gen2:24 is a narratation. "This verse contains the comment which the narrator makes upon the words of the man in Genesis 2:23. The word “therefore” introduces his inference. As in Genesis 10:9, Genesis 26:33, Genesis 32:32, a sentence beginning with “therefore” supplies the application, or relation, of the ancient narrative to later times. "(Cambridge Bible for Schools and Colleges). Also, Barnes' Notes on the Bible said, "But it seems more probable that this sentence is the reflection of the inspired author on the special mode in which the female was formed from the male. Such remarks of the writer are frequently introduced by the word "therefore" (על־כן kēn-‛al). It is designed to inculcate on the race that was to spring from them the inviolable sanctity of the conjugal relation." 
 
A few of scholars believe that Gen2:24 were from Adam. Barnes' Notes on the Bible states, "These might be the words of the first man Genesis 2:24. As he thoroughly understood the relation between himself and the woman, there is no new difficulty in conceiving him to become acquainted at the same time with the relationship of son to father and mother, which was in fact only another form of that in which the newly-formed woman stood to himself. The latter is really more intimate and permanent than the former, and naturally therefore takes its place, especially as the practical of the filial tie, - that of being trained to maturity, - is already accomplished, when the conjugal one begins."
 
Some believe that whether Gen2:24 are from Adam or Moses cannot be certain.  Matthew Poole's Commentary: "These are the words of Moses by Divine instinct, or his inference from Adam’s words."  Benson Commentary: "It appears by Matthew 19:4-5, that it was God himself who said here, a man must leave all his relations to cleave to his wife; but whether he spake this by Moses or by Adam, is uncertain."
 
The only agreement is that Gen2:24 are words inspired by the Spirit of God. Ellicott's Commentary: "the inspired narrator was moved by the Spirit of God" Gill's Exposition of the Entire Bible: "Therefore shall a man leave his father, and his mother,.... These are thought by some to be the words of Moses, inferring from the above fact, what ought to be among men; and by others, the words of Adam under divine inspiration, as the father of mankind instructing his sons what to do, and foretelling what would be done in all succeeding ages: though they rather seem to be the words of God himself, by whom marriage was now instituted;"
 
Dispite of the discrepancy, it is an interesting and strange phenomenon that all English translations of the Bible unanimously exclude the Gen2:24 out of the Adam's quotation marks. Only the Literal Standard Version (https://biblehub.com/lsv/genesis/2.htm) differs from others in that, it excludes not only Gen2:24, but also the second half of Gen2:23. "and the man says, 'This at last! Bone of my bone, and flesh of my flesh!' For this is called Woman, for this has been taken from Man;” (Gen2:23 LSV) Three relatively independant statements are logically related in Gen2:23-24: (1) this is bone of my bone, and flesh of my flesh; (2) this is called woman because of (כִּ֥י) that; (3) for this reason (עַל־כֵּן֙), man shall leave father and mother to unit woman to become one flesh. Due to the continuity of the reasoning clause, LSV believes that, if Gen2:24 was excluded, then the second half part of Gen2:23 should also be excluded.  
 
Who is 'Father' or 'Mother'?
 
With regard to the Mosaic laws of the marriage related to Gen2:24, when focusing on the unit and indivisiblilty between husband and wife, the quotation issue seems not important. However, when concerning about how to exposition the biblical meaning of 'father' and 'mother', the quotation issue becomes crucial.  
 
If Gen2:24 were from Moses (narrator) inspired by God, then the concept of 'father and mother' would be limited inside the comunicating context between Moses and his audence or readers in the culture. To leave parents would be simply like a command of God. It is like what God said to Abraham,"Leave ... your father's household and go to the land I will show you." (Gen12:1 NIV). Jesus once replied to a disciple filial to his father, 'follow me and let the dead to bury the dead' (Mat8:22). Regarding about mother, Jesus once said, "Who is my mother?... For whoever does the will of my Father in heaven is ... my mother."(Mat12:48-50 NIV). In the cultral context, when thinking father and mother geneaologically or biologically, father and mother would refer to the parents' house, the hometown, the credle of civilication of the world. What God wanted man to do is to leave the old world to seek a new world. Such process is like what Jesus said to be reborn in John 4 to unit the bridegroom who is Jesus Christ to enter into the kingdom of heaven. There is no exegetical or theological confliction issue in this case, however, the original biblical meaning about 'father and mother' can hardly be expositioned. 
 
Who invented 'father, 'mother', 'man' and 'woman'?
 
If Gen2:24 were from mouth of Adam, then the concept of 'father', 'mother',  'man' and 'woman' would have to come from the brain of Adam. The description of how Adam gave names of animals (Gen2:19-20), was inserted into Gen2, right before the verses of how woman was created (Gen2:21-22), as well as the first time appearance of  'father', 'mother', 'man' (ish) and 'woman' (ishah) in the Scripture,  all of these suggest that these terms were originally named by Adam under inspiration of God. This context is critical to exposition the biblical meaning of 'father' and 'mother.'
 
Whom did 'father' and 'mother' refer to?
 
Adam did not have father and mother. Adam is not the father of Eve even though Eve came from the rib of Adam. Also, Adam did not have any offspring at the time when he first time met Eve -- Adam and Eve were not a father and mother yet until they sinned. Adam might observe parents among animals, but Adam had never seen any human father and mother before. The words 'father and mother' from Adam's mouth had to infer to the Creator God at that moment.
Adam was directly created and educated by God (Gen2:16-17). Adam knew of God more than Eve, and super more than anyone else among his offsprings of all his generations in human history. With experiences of naming, being inspired by the Spirit of God, Adam had capability to give names 'father' and 'mother' for God.
God called the first man He made 'Adam' (Gen2:7, Gen3:9). But God did not tell Adam the name of God. What God told Adam is not to eat the forbidden fruit. God did not reveal His name 'I am who I am' (אֶֽהְיֶה אֲשֶׁר אֶֽהְיֶה) until Moses asked him (Exo3:14). Adam did not know how to call God. However, God gave Adam the right of freedom of speech. The only restriction for him is not to eat the forbidden fruit. Adam could name all things that he did not know including himself and Eve. Among creatures, only Adam had such privilage right from God to give names for God. This shows a love of God. 
 
Are 'father' and 'mother' two in one?
 
Adam knew God more than anyone else. The Scriptures reiterates that there is only one God. There are many reasons that Adam called God 'father and mother.'  First, calling God 'father' and 'mother' does not necessarily mean two persons, nor one personal God with two gender characters. Instead it was a way to distinguish the callers between 'man' (ish) and 'woman' (ishah). Adam's self-consciousness was not strong enough yet. Adam even was not able to distinguish clearly between himself and Eve since they had no distinct names but shared the identical flesh and bones with the breath of Yehweh God. It was a great invetion for Adam to be able to call himself 'man' (ish) and her ('esha'). Adam did not know how and who Eve was created because he was put in sleep at that time. God led her to Adam but did not told him anything about her (Gen2:22). It could be based upon the eye contacts or other kinds of mutual comunication that made Adam to believe that she was 'bones of my bones, flesh of my flesh'. It is likely that Adam refer 'father' to the creation of Adam, 'mother' to the creation of Eve. Adam called 'father' and 'mother' to honor the one Creator God for two masterpieces of creation.
Second, the pair of terms 'father' and 'mother' could be used to distinct between 'man' (ish) and 'woman' (esha) for the difference between Adam and Eve. Adam called himself man (esh) and God 'father' (aba), Adam also defined that Eve should be called 'woman' and Eve should call God 'mother'. Adam thought that it could help God to distinguish between him and her when both called God. Adam is probably the first one to call God with different names to distinguish between Adam and Eve: 'man' (ish) called God 'father', 'woman' (ishah) called God mother. This phenonmenon is common in cultural and social history: Islam called God 'Allah', some Hinduism call God 'Shiva', Chinese called God 'Shen', etc.. Having different names of God does not mean that there are many different types of gods, instead, the different names of God reflect the different sorts of man how they identify themself. Among numerous names, 'Father' was the earliest name Adam gave to God. 
Third, Hebrew 'Yehweh Elohim' could be a hint why Adam called God 'father and mother'. By comparison bewteen Genesis 1 and 2, it can be found that Genesis 1 uses single word 'Elohim' to present God, whereas Genesis 2 uses two words 'Yehweh Elohim' to present God. Yehweh and Elohim are widely used solitoary elsewhere to represent God in the Scriptures. It is only in Genesis 2 that both are always used together.  This is not an accidence or coincidence. Genesis 1 focuses on the 6-day creation of the world, Genesis 2 focuses on the creation of man (adam): Adam and Eve, as well as the relationship of man (esh) and woman (esha), father and mother.  'Yehweh' and 'Elohim' are two terms for one God. Whereas, the word 'adam' is both a collective name for man and a proper name for the first created individual Adam; man (ish) and woman (isha) are two names of two types of one man (adam);  father and mother are two names from two types of man for God. It is through the pair of meanings and the pair of words that forms the likeness between the image of God (Yehweh Elohim) and the man (adam and Adam, Adam and Eve, man and woman, father and mother). It was also likely that Adam call 'father' and 'mother' under the infleunce of the 'Yehweh Elohim'. 
Moreover, Yehweh and Eve are closely related both in pronounciation and meanings in Hebrew 'to be' and 'live' (see table). The name of 'Eve' was given by Adam after the fall, which means 'the mother of all the living' (3:20).  Eve was not merely the mother of man any more but all the living. For Adam, Eve was equilalent to a name of a god. 'We cannot understand the history of Eve without seeing her as a deposed Creator-Goddess.'[bookTemplate197book, p3]  Genesis 2 could not tell the reason or meaming for both 'father' and 'mother' like the word 'woman' did. It was the usage of 'mother' in Eve's name change from 'woman' to 'Eve' that shed the some light about the original biblical meaning of  'mother'. 
On the other line, the connection between Adam and 'father' is indirectly revealed through Adam's first born child, the murderer Cain's family tree (Gen4:20, Gen4:21). A clearer meaning of 'father' is demonstrated when God (Elohim) changed the name from Abram to Abraham, Abraham means 'the father of many nations' (Gen17:4). It is noticeable that Yehweh and Elohim interplays in Gen17. Yehwey is used in Gen17:1. Elohim is used in Gen17:3 when He made the changes of the name. It is through the changes of names of Eve and Abram by Adam and God respectively that the difference between 'father' and 'mother' can be distinct. It can be seen that there are two lines of connections: (1) the connection between 'mother' and Eve, the mother of all the living and Yehweh; (2) the connection between 'father' and Abram, the father of nations and Elohim. These two lines of connections underlined the connection relationship between God (Yehweh Elohim) and 'father and mother.'  -- 'mother' infers to Yehweh; 'father' infers to Elohim. In another word, the distinction between 'father' and 'mother' is a similar to the distinction between the name of Yehweh and Elohim.
 
'Male' or 'Female'
 
In Genesis 1, God created 'male' and 'female' in the second step of the creation of man, but the distinct between 'male' and 'female' is not clear in Genesis 1. Although Genesis 2 clearly demostrated the difference between man and woman, and Genesis 3 indirectly articulated the difference between 'father' and 'mohter', the differrence between 'male' and 'female' cannot be linked to the difference between man and woman, or father and mother. "male and female' are not even used in Genesis 2 and 3.  This shows that Adam could distinct between man (ish) and woman (ishah), father and mother, but he could not understand the meaning of Male and Femal, nor could he distinct between "Male' and 'Female'.  'Male' and 'Female' are names used by God to distinct two types of man. The unknowing of the meanings of male and female could help to prevent Adam from misuse of 'male' and 'female' to link to God when he cried out 'father and mother'. 'Male' and 'Female' cannot be applied to describe one God.
On the other hand, Adam could be associated with man and father but not male; Eve could be associated with woman and mother but not female. In another word, 'male' and 'female' cannot be associated to man (ish) and woman (eshah), or father and mother respectively until later in a cultural context after the fall. This indicates that Adam and Eve are qualified to be called 'man'(ish) and 'woman' (esha). But just like Adam and Eve are not qualified to be called 'father' and 'mother', Adam and Eve are not qualified to be called 'male' or 'female' yet.
There exists a consistency among the different pairs between Adam and Eve, man and woman, or father and mother -- their differences are not based upon 'male' or 'female' characters, nor based upon the gender or sex, instead, they are based on the privilage in the creation and duties or responisibilities in missionary of God. Hence, the likeness between the image of God and man is not based upon the gender but based upon the privilage in creation and missionary of God. It is a misuse to associated 'male' with 'man' (ish) and 'father", and 'female' with 'woman' (esha) and 'motner' in human culture after the fall. It is only after Jesus came into the world, those of brothers and sisters who can follow Him to accomplish the mission of God can be truely called male and female.      
 
The Beauty of the First Love Story
 
Meeting a creature similar to himself, could be the most exciting event for Adam in his life after enduring the loneliness and helpless (Gen2:22b) for long time -- at least, naming the numerious animals is not an easy task for Adam, it's better to have a helper to speak with. For Adam, Eve is more beautiful than scenes of rivers and mountains, more precious than gold or treasures (Gen2:10-14). This could lead him to speak out the the surprising words. These words become the most precious words for people today to better understand Adam before his fall.  
From Gen2:23, it can be found that, when Adam first time saw Eve, he could recognize her  without the need of God's introduction. This shows that, although Adam could not know how Eve was created in detail, since he was sleeping at that time, Adam clearly knew who created her. Particulary, Adam could understand the reason why she was created -- she was created for him -- he would no longer be thirsty and lonely in spirit any more. Adam could feel that God could understand his heart. At that moment, Adam could be filled with the love of God, mixed with the love of Eve and with the joy in his heart -- the first and the strongest emotion in humam beings. Being inspired by the Holy Spirit, as a first response in the world in thanksgiving for God, Adam cried out 'father' and 'mother' from his mouth promptly, and probably with the first human tears. 'Father' expressed the great thanks of Adam to God; 'mother' represented the thanks for and of Eve to God, since Eve could hardly speak yet. "Because you are sons, God sent the Spirit of his Son into our hearts (Adam) , the Spirit who calls out, 'Abba (αββα), Father.' " (Gal4:6 NIV) From Hebrew word father (אָב), to Greek father (αββα), to English 'father'  or 'Abba'  not only the meaning, but also the sound or IPA (The International Phonetic Alphabet) keeps the same. The words became the terms and are used globally until today. 
 
 
Gen 2:24 states that man should leave father and mother. It is consistent to what God commanded Abraham to leave father's house to go to Canaan (Gen12:1). It is also consistent to Jesus's teaching. When a disciple said to Jesus to first let him go and bury his father, Jesus told him, "Follow me, and let the dead bury their own dead." (Mat8:22 NIV). Jesus once said, "Who is my mother, and who are my brothers? For whoever does the will of my Father in heaven is my brother and sister and mother."(Mat12:48-50 NIV). Although the context of father and mother is different, from Adam to Abraham, to disciples and Jesus, they all have a common goal to leave father and mother: to unit wife, church, Jesus, to accomplish the mission of God. From this perspective, Gen2:24 is a prototype and a prediction of Jesus' great commition:"Therefore go and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit, and teaching them to obey everything I have commanded you. And surely I am with you always, to the very end of the age." (Mat28:19-20)  What Adam and Jesus talked about 'father' and 'mother' is identical but different from the cultural flesh concept of the world.
Gen2:24 indicated that it is man (ish), rather than woman (esha), who should or would leave father and mother, and should or would unit woman (esha) rather than man (ish). Adam could distinguish between man (adam) in 2:23 and man (ish). God called the first man Adam, but Adam first time to call himself man (ish). By saying so, it has multiple significances. (1) Adam grew up -- he did not think himself a generic man (adam) but a specific kind of man (ish) that is different from woman (esha).  (2) Adam knew the defference between man (esh) and womon (eshah) over the privilage both in the order of creation but also the in the leading role of missionary of God. Adam considered himself as man (ish). It means that he had admitted and accepted the privilage and all duties and responsibilities that man (ish) should take. (3) The imperfect conjugation of the verb  leave (יַֽעֲזָב) and cleave (וְדָבַק) could express not only the potential actions but also the desirability that Adam was willing to take duties to please God. It was the first response of the love of God in human history.  (4) As the first created man (adam) and the first man (esh), by calling God 'father', Adam established a sonship relationship with God and became the first son of God. Since then, Adam was not the same as other creatures any more in the world. (5)  With the manship and sonship, calling God 'father and mother' was a signature and seal under the third party authority and testimony to allow God to legalize the husband-wife relationship between Adam and Eve. Gen2:24 is more than a narrative, command or prediction, but is more like an oath, swear, contract or a divine ordinance in a wedding ceremony in front of Go. (6) Adam should not call God 'mother'. Adam's calling of 'mother' was a supplication for Eve. It showed that Adam had determined to protect and take over Eve -- Eve does not belong to God but belong to Adam. 
 
God loved Adam and permitted man (ish) to leave Him to cleave with woman in order to become father and mother. It is likely that God used Adam's words to build up the first marriage contitution. With imperfect conjugation that can present the future tense, Gen2:24 is also like a prediction that Adam and Eve would be the first father and mother according to the likeness of God.
Although Adam actively called God 'father' and humblly called God 'mother' more or less because of  Eve, Adam well played the role of the first born, the elder and the husband and the head of Eve -- Adam can be called a real man (ish) and he is deserved to be called the first son of God. However, Adam eventually failed his swear, and failed to please God. Adam could not be a perfect model until Jesus came into the world to make it up on the cross. It is through the last son of God, Jesus, who eventually pleased God 'This is my beloved Son.' (Mat)
 
It is inarguable that Adam loves Eve. Before the fall, in Genesis 2, Adam initially used the name 'isha' (woman) to call his companion all the time. The name 'Eve' was given after the fall and after they were judged (Gen3:20) but before they were driven out of Eden. The word 'Eve' was also the last word that Adam could give in Eden. Before being driven out of Eden, Adam gave his companion the title Eve to express his loyalty and love of Eve -- no matter where they went, the first love would not change.  'Eve' could also a name for both of them to keep in memory of his first day of the first love. The connection between the name 'Eve' and 'Yehweh' could symolize the immortal love as a contrast to their death. Such love is ordained by God. It was the love of Adam that led him to had three children later in the cursed land (Gen4:1-2). Although Adam betrayed (or frankly spoke out) Eve in front of God during the time when he was interrogated, Adam still loved Eve in his heart until his death at 930 years old -- Adam did not have any other wifes in his life. Although Adam committed the sin to eat the forbidden fruit, Adam's marriage from the beginning to the end is deserved to be praised among human beings forever.
The love story of Adam was the first and most divine love story between the first couple in humanity. However, because Adam and Eve ate the forbidden fruit, the flesh and bones were toxified inside out. No any other love story can compare with it.  All human loves are replaced with the illusionary driven by chemichal power more or less full of the adultery. Jesus quoted Gen2:24 verse to remind people to re-focus on the very original love and initial marriage rather than to think how to breach the Mosaic Laws (Mat19:5,  Mak10:6-9). Jesus took over the love of Adam and replace it with a greater love on the cross. Paul pointed out that the verse Gen2:24 is significant more than the marriage: "For this reason a man will leave his father and mother and be united to his wife, and the two will become one flesh. This is a profound mystery -- but I am talking about Christ and the church." (Eph5:31-32 NIV) "But he who unites himself with the Lord is one with him in spirit." (1Co6:17 NIV)  
If Gen2:24 were from mouth of Adam, the love story of Adam was not over. Because the love story of Adam had a limitation -- it explosed a critical issue: Adam shifted the weight center of the love from God to Eve. After Adam met Eve, Adam fell in love with Eve. After Adam had a helper Eve --  he did not need to fully count on God. As his relationship with Eve became closer and closer, Adam thought he could leave God to unite with Eve. Between Eve and God, Adam would rather to choose Eve. Eve was the individible part of the life of Adam. Adam could no longer live without Eve. Eve became a god rather a helper of Adam. From the unique proper name 'Eve' given by Adam, which means the mother of all living things (Gen3:20), it can be found that Adam at last shifted his life center of the world view from God to Eve -- Adam put Eve in a position above God: It was Eve who was the oiginator or creator of all living things rather than God or 'mother' Adam called God for Eve. Such issue was corrected by God through the changes of the name of Abram to Abraham, Abraham means the father of many nations.(Gen17:1)
Superficially, it was the fault of Eve who ate the forbidden fruit.  Essentially the fault came from the inner heart of Adam. He failed to protect, teach, correct and guide his wife the same as how God pastored him, instead he believed in wife and followed the words and actions of wife and even to be against God to eat the forbbiden fruit. Adam said to God "The woman whom thou gavest to be with me, she gave me of the tree, and I did eat." (Gen3:12 KJV) Adam's word was not merely an excuse, it also authentically exposed his inner heart: Adam considered the gift of God as God. Jesus passed all complicated laws and pin pointed out that highest of the laws is " 'Love the Lord your God with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your mind.' This is the first and greatest commandment." (Mat22:37-38 NIV) "And the second is like it: 'Love your neighbor as yourself.' All the Law and the Prophets hang on these two commandments." (Mat22:39-40 NIV) More accurately speaking, Adam is the only one who committed the first not second of the top two commandments. Eating the forbidden fruit is not the reason but the result of sin. Genesis 2 shows that the creation of man was finished but not yet. After Adam and Eve were created and united as oneness (Gen2:24), the creation of man was done but not yet. Paul said, "So it is written: "The first man Adam became a living being" ; the last Adam, a lifegiving spirit." (1Co15:45 NIV). Jesus came to the world not to judge who was fault. Any one who believe in Him will have the eternal love greater than Adam.
In the Gospels, Jesus called God as his 'Father'. This is not something new that He invented. Whom Jesus called 'father' is identical to whom Adam called 'father'. A goal Jesus came to the world is to restore the original meaning of 'father' that Adam invented in Gen2:24 before the fall. It is through the restoration of the original meaming of 'father', the etymology issue of 'mother' can be solved. 'mother' is not Mary, nor Eve, nor goddees nor heavenly mother. 'mother' originally intended to point to God by Adam representing the woman. Whereas the original meaning of 'mother', defined by Adam after God's judgement will remain until today (Gen3:20). By calling 'Father' on the cross to forgive us, Jesus accomplished the 'father' contextualization.
 
 
Gen 2:24 states that man should leave father and mother. If Gen2:24 came from the tongue of Moses, it would be subject to conflict the human virtue of parent filial responsibility that is related to the fifth of the Ten Commandments in the OT: “Honor thy father and thy mother.” However, Gen2:24 is more consistent with Jesus' teaching. Conclusion
 
Whether Gen2:24 came from the tongue of Adam or Moses (the narrator) is uncertain. Most scholars believe that Gen2:24 is from the writer. Barnes' Notes on the Bible:
These might be the words of the first man Genesis 2:24. As he thoroughly understood the relation between himself and the woman, there is no new difficulty in conceiving him to become acquainted at the same time with the relationship of son to father and mother, which was in fact only another form of that in which the newly-formed woman stood to himself. The latter is really more intimateand permanent than the former, and naturally therefore takes its place, especially as the practical of the filial tie, - that of being trained to maturity, - is already accomplished, when the conjugal one begins.
But it seems more probable that this sentence is the reflection of the inspired author on the special mode in which the female was formed from the male. Such remarks of the writer are frequently introduced by the word "therefore" (על־כן kēn-‛al). It is designed to inculcate on the race that was to spring from them the inviolable sanctity of the conjugal relation.
In the primeval wedlock one man was joined to one woman only for life. Hence, in the marriage relation the animal is subordinate to the rational. The communication of ideas; the cherishing of the true, the right, the good; the cultivation of the social affections; the spontaneous outflow of mutual good offices; the thousand nameless little thoughts, looks, words, and deeds that cheer the brow and warm the heart; the common care of children, servants, and dependents; the constant and heartfelt worship of the Father of all, constitute the main ends and joys of the married state.
After the exclamation of the man on contemplating the woman, as bone of his bones and flesh of his flesh, and therefore physically, intellectually, and morally qualified to be his mate, we may suppose immediately to follow the blessing of man, and the general endowment of himself and the animals with the fruits of the soil as recorded in the preceding chapter Genesis 1:28-30. The endowment of man embraces every tree in which is the fruit of a tree yielding seed. This general grant was of course understood by man to exclude the tree of the knowledge of good and evil, which was excepted, if not by its specific nature, yet by the previous command given to man. This command we find was given before the formation of the woman, and therefore sometime before the events recorded in the second and third clauses of Genesis 1:27. Hence, it preceded the blessing and the endowment. It was not special, however, to the tree of the knowledge of good and evil to be intended for other purposes than the food of man, as there are very many other trees that afford no proper nutriment to man. The endowment, therefore, refers to such trees as were at the same time nutritive and not expressly and previously forbidden.
This chapter is occupied with the "generations, issues or products of the skies and the land," or, in other words, of the things created in the six days. It is the meet preface to the more specific history of man, as it records his constitution, his provision, his moral and intellectual cultivation, and his social perfection. It brings us up to the close of the sixth day. As the Creator pronounced a sentence of approbation on all that he had made at the end of that day, we have reason to believe that no moral derangement had yet taken place in man's nature.

Cambridge Bible for Schools and Colleges
24. Therefore shall a man, &c.] This verse contains the comment which the narrator makes upon the words of the man in Genesis 2:23. The word “therefore” introduces his inference. As in Genesis 10:9, Genesis 26:33, Genesis 32:32, a sentence beginning with “therefore” supplies the application, or relation, of the ancient narrative to later times.
It is the man who is to leave “father and mother,” not “the woman.” Some compare the story in Jdg 15:1, where the woman remains with her family or clan, and Samson comes to live with her. This feature has been thought to illustrate the primitive usage of “the matriarchate.” But it is unlikely that the Hebrew narrative would contain a reference to such conditions.
Instead of “shall leave,” the full force of the tense in the Hebrew would be given by “doth leave” and “cleaveth.” The sanctity of marital relations is thus referred back to the very birthday of human society, being based on a principle laid down before the Fall.
The relation of the man to his wife is proclaimed to be closer than that to his father and mother. By the words, “shall cleave unto his wife … one flesh,” is asserted the sanctity of marriage. Polygamy is not definitely excluded; but the principle of monogamy seems to be implied in the words “cleave” and “shall be one flesh”: and this principle is upheld by the prophets as the ideal of marriage, in their representation of the relation of Jehovah and Israel under the metaphor of the married state.
