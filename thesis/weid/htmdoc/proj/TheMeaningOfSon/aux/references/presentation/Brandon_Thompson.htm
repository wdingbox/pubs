How do you plan to pursue this topic without "preoccupied knowledge about 'the son' from human culture?" By human culture, do you mean your own, ancient Near Eastern culture, and/or Second Temple period culture?

What is the purpose of studying kinship language aside from the use of 'sonship' if your goal is to understand a 'sonship' relationship?

How do you think Genesis 1-5 can inform the phrase 'Son of Man' when this phrase does not appear and, if you argue that the concept behind the phrase is present, how do you reconcile 2TP understandings of this phrase that would have been in the NT's authors' minds (and if not theirs, then their audiences')?

 

I think you can go in two directions. If your goal is to understand Genesis' use of familial language with the intent of understanding the structure of the family after creation and in the immediate aftermath of the fall, then your study of these familial terms would work (and this would be an amazing topic, by the way).

If your goal is to understand the meaning of the phrases 'Son of Man' and 'Son of God,' then I do not think you are going to get anywhere studying these terms in Genesis 1-5. The primary issues are that these phrases do not appear in Genesis 1-5 and that human culture significantly impacts all the language of the Bible. Whenever God communicates, he communicates in that culture's 'cultural river' (this phrase is one used by John H. Walton). As such, if your goal is to understand the phrases 'Son of Man' and 'Son of God,' then you need to delve within the culture that provided those terms to see what these terms communicated in that culture. This would mean studying Ezekiel and Daniel (and their accompanying Babylon backgrounds) and 1 Enoch and other Second Temple period literature (and how these phrases develop in this corpus in light of the previous biblical backgrounds).

Alternatively, you could take aspects of both of these and make a great topic, just not in the way you formulated it.

If you are set on studying Genesis and Jesus, then I would recommend studying how ancient Near Eastern peoples understood the concept of divine sonship and how the biblical authors took this cultural concept and applied it to Adam. As notions of divine sonship continued into the Roman era (cf. the emperor), you could then look at the cultural concept of divine sonship in the Greco-Roman era and see how the NT applies it to Jesus, while at the same time casting Adam and Jesus as a type and antitype since Adam was still understood in divine sonship terms (though in a different cultural context, so you would still need to trace the development of this idea across 2TP literature).

Here are some resources that can guide you in your thinking about culture:

For ANE: Walton, John H. Ancient Near Eastern Thought and the Old Testament: Introducing the Conceptual World of the Hebrew Bible. 2d ed. Grand Rapids, MI: Baker Academic, 2018.

For 2TP: Green, Joel B., and Lee Martin McDonald (eds.). The World of the New Testament: Cultural, Social, and Historical Contexts. Grand Rapids, MI: Baker Academic, 2017.

If you decide that any of the topics I provided above would be a good topic to pursue, let me know and I can send some other resources your way. I would encourage you to reach out to Dr. Noonan (Old Testament) or Dr. Naylor (New Testament) for some guidance on these topics. They have both spent quite a bit of time in this area. I hope that all of this has been helpful and I look forward to seeing where you take this idea!
