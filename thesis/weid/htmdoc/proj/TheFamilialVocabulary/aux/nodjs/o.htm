The Possible "Son" Contextualization
in Ancient Chinese Documents

Abstraction:

Everyone knows the meaning of the word "son". But for its literally equivalent word (Zi 子) (Zi) in Chinese language, both of its usage and meaning, are still an unresolved mystery in its ancient history. For, example, why Zi (子) is commonly used in ancient books and the sage's name, like, KongZi 孔子 (Confucius), MenZi 孟子 (Mencius), LaoZi 老子 (Laoism/Daoism), etc. This paper applied the word frequency-spectrum analysis along with the category-periodic comparison method to study the roles and characteristics of the word Zi in Chinese ancient documents. The research found that the rank and the density of Zi (子) in some specific categories of ancient books appears "unreasonably" high in time of BC. But it sharply went down and remained stable in time of AD. The discovery, that the similarity of the meaning of the high frequent prefix and suffix of Zi (子) during its high frequency period is very similar to the words that is used to describe the Messiah both in the OT and the NT, provides an important clue that could lead to the possible sonship contextualization -- the original meaning of "son" (Zi 子) in ancient China is likely to point to the first advent of the Son of God--Jesus Christ. While struggling about the connection between the East and the West, this study also can trigger Christians, missionaries and theologians today to further rethink the contextualizational meaning of the "son" in our daily life -- why Jesus Christ is described as the Son of man and the Son of God.

Key Words: son, Zi (子), contextualization, inter-culture, Chinese pre-Qin documents.

Three major tasks must be done in this research: A) to select the Chinese documents for research; B) to classify the collected documents; C)  to calculate their frequency-spectrum and to generate a category-periodic table. 

  1. The Collection of Documents

    Following principles are considered during the data collection process.
    1. The pre-Qin documents:
      The pre-Qin documents are the primary target in this research. The term pre-Qin is also called pre-China. The word "China" is a western name given for "Qin". According to the history study, Qin is the first dynasty (221-206 BC) in history that unified all states into one nation. Hence, the term pre-Qin literally means the history before 221 BC. However, before around seventh century BC, there is no any paper-like or bamboo-strip lengthy document -- all the archeologists can find  today are just pieces or groups of characters on bronzes or bones. Therefore, the term pre-Qin of documents actually refer to a narrow period of time between the seventh century BC and the second century BC. 

      The term pre-Qin first appears in the book of HanShu in the first century AD. The reason that the term is widely accepted and used in history is probably not because of the unification of the warring states, but because of a key factor that all significant philosophical documents suddenly appeared during that short time period. After that, for more than two thousand of years, there is no any one that can compare with them. 

      In theory, the pre-Qin documents are the foundation of the Chinese culture. All basic concepts and terms about the moral, politics, life, worldview and faith, etc. come from the pre-Qin document. The pre-Qin documents are the bases of all other documents and influencing people even until today. It is the pre-Qin documents that cultivate the people's spiritual mind that can distinguish themselves to others in the world.
      From the missiological view point, the pre-Qin documents is a stronghold for the missionary of China. 

      In practice, the term of pre-Qin documents is hard to follow, since there is no consensus agreement on the book list for the pre-Qin documents in history.  The pre-Qin documents is actually a concept of fuzzy set. This paper primarily follows the list of Chinese Text Project and considers about fifty of those with the label of year before 221 BC as pre-Qin documents. 

    2. The post-Qin documents:
      To be able to find the characteristics of the pre-Qin documents, it is necessary to compare them with other documents written after 221 BC, namely the post-Qin documents. Since the number of the Chinese ancient documents are extremely huge, this research only selected about hundred of them to balance between the pre-Qin and post-Qin, the important and unimportant, philosophical and non-philosophical documents. This paper selected about a hundred of documents that mainly from the Chinese Text Project (ctext.org).

    3. The biblical and non-biblical Chinese documents:
      The ultimate goal of the paper is to examine the Chinese documents from a biblical view to find a way for local cultural contextualization. Hence, three Chinese versions of the Bible are selected for comparison study. To enrich the comparison, the Chinese version of the Quran and Mormon are also included in the list.

  2. The Classification of the Collected Documents
    Two types of classification method are used in the research: the time based and the content based classification.
    1. The Chronicle Classification
      Any document can be classified according to the time when it was written, or the book was discovered, or the time that its content refers to. A most useful information is the date when the book was written or published. However, since none of the written date of the existing pre-Qin documents can be confirmed or verified, the time used here is a rough estimation based on the discovery, content, traditional legend, or reference from other 'known' books.    

      1. The traditional Chinese dynasty classification:
        Chinese tradition prefers to classify the date of document according to the name its associated dynasty. There are about two dozens of dynasties in China. Some of them may have an overlap in time.  To simplify this, this paper import the Western pre-Modern concept, and to classify the collected documents into four periods: PreQin (before 221 BC), QinHan (221 BC - 220 AD) pre-Modern (220-1911 AD) and post-modern (1911--).
         
      2. The biblical classification.
        Not all scholars believe that the most important and influential Chinese documents are the pre-Qin documents. For example, Dr. Donald Sturgeon uses QinHan (221 BC - 220 AD) as the boundary of the Chinese history in his Chinese Text Project (ctext.org). His method expands the pre-Qin documents for 400 years from the year before 221 BC to 220 AD.  Noticing that the middle point between 221 BC and 220 AD is the common era, this paper introduced the biblical classification method -- to classify any Chinese document as part of either BC or AD.

      This paper will follow the popular date information. When a written date of document is arguable and uncertain, the paper will take an available one in order to go on.  While following the traditional dynasty based classification method, this paper also combined it with the biblical classification method. See Table-1.

    2. The Content Classification:
      Based its content, this paper follows two existing types of classification: people-oriented and the object-oriented classification. 
      1. Chinese traditional people-oriented classification.
        The people-oriented classification method is to classify an ideology, thought or content of document according to the founder and its group of followers, namely the family or school.  For example, KongZi (Confucius) and MenZi (Mencius) are consistent in teaching, so they are classified into Ru family; LaoZi and ZhuangZi are similar, they are gropped to a family of Dao. The people-oriented classification method can be found in pre-Qin documents. For example, book of 荀子 XunZi contains criticism about the plagiarism in Ru family. The people-oriented classification can be considered as one of the unique features of the pre-Qin culture. After pre-Qin, for over two thousand years, almost no new family or school ever appeared in history.

        A critical issue in people-oriented classification is that the definition of the school or family cannot be clearly defined. For example, today, the term Ru family is usually defined as a system of philosophical and ethical teachings or thoughts founded by KongZi and his followers. But what the  Ru family exactly teaches about is still arguable and uncertain. Character Ru cannot be found in the bronzes or oracle bones. The meaning of character Ru is also obscure.  The first Chinese dictionary (Shuowen jiezi 說文解字), being dated back to first century CE, says, “Ru means gentle and is how men of techniques are called” 儒, 柔也朮士之稱The scholar Liang Cai pointed out that “KongZi (Confucius) did not create Ru-family (Confucianism). Actually, no one ever thought so.” Liang Cai pointed out that it is a puzzle in history that people were turned away to focus on the study of the Ru family (or Confucianism).

        In spite of the defect of the people-oriented classification, there is no better way to well organized the pre-Qin document so far. How to biblically classify the pre-Qin documents will be a challenging work that missionaries need to do in future. At the moment, this paper will follow the existing people-oriented classification.

      2. The object-oriented classification method.
        The object-oriented classification is to categorize the books according to the topic, or ideology. For example, the history, medicine, military etc. It is more explicit than the people-oriented classification. 

      This paper will combine the traditional people-oriented and object-oriented classification method, and call them as a category.
